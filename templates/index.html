The Rich History of Kanheri

The Kanheri Caves constitute a group of rock-cut monuments located in the forests of the Sanjay Gandhi National Park on the island of Salsette in Mumbai, India. The caves were built in phases from the 1st century BCE to the 10th century CE.

The name Kanheri comes from the Sanskrit word 'Krishnagiri', which means black mountain. The site is located on a massive stretch of basalt rock, also known as the Deccan Trap.

The caves served as Buddhist monastic residences and were centers of learning, meditation, and worship for nearly a millennium.

Cave Architecture

The Kanheri complex includes more than 100 caves of varying sizes, each with unique features. The most remarkable are:

Chaityagriha (Prayer Halls)

Large worship halls with stupas, featuring detailed carvings and Buddhist inscriptions from different periods.

Viharas (Monasteries)

Residential quarters for the monks with stone platforms for sleeping and meditation spaces.

Water Management System

An elaborate system of cisterns, channels, and dams designed to collect rainwater.

Buddhist Artwork

Numerous Buddha images, Bodhisattva figures, and intricate carvings depicting Buddhist themes.
